1. ONE-PARAGRAPH SUMMARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This is a small-scale, reproducible empirical study of whether CONTENT-BASED
SPARSE ATTENTION can preserve associative recall — the known failure mode of
sub-quadratic sequence models — while keeping its selection mechanism itself
sub-quadratic. The testbed is MQAR (Multi-Query Associative Recall) from the
HazyResearch "Zoology" harness, run on tiny models (millions of parameters) on
free-tier GPUs. So far I have (a) reproduced the Zoology recall-vs-state-size
gap between attention and gated-convolution models, (b) shown that DeepSeek-NSA
style sparse attention rides the full-attention recall ceiling (~0.99) at
matched capacity, and (c) designed and implemented a novel HIERARCHICAL
(two-level tree) block selector that reduces the selector's cost from O(n^2)
to ~O(n^1.5), which is the paper's intended core contribution. The length-sweep
experiment that tests this selector is coded, hardened, and ready to run.


2. MOTIVATION AND CONTEXT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- The associative-recall wall: linear-attention / SSM / convolutional models
  compress history into a fixed-size state and demonstrably fail exact
  key->value recall (Zoology, Arora et al.). Full attention solves recall but
  is O(n^2).
- In May 2026 the startup Subquadratic ("SubQ", $29M seed, Miami) launched a
  claimed 12M-token-context LLM built on "SSA" (Subquadratic Sparse Attention):
  per-query content-based top-k selection followed by EXACT attention over the
  selected subset, claimed O(n*k). All benchmark numbers are vendor-run; there
  is NO paper and no independent reproduction. The field's loudest complaint
  is precisely "no paper."
- The nearest public cousin is DeepSeek's NSA (Native Sparse Attention). But
  NSA's selector is itself quadratic: every query scores ALL compressed blocks
  (O(n^2 / block_size)) — the complexity is moved, not removed.
- Opportunity: an honest, independent, small-scale study of whether content-
  based sparse selection preserves recall — and whether the selector can be
  made genuinely sub-quadratic without losing recall — is cheap, tractable,
  currently missing from the literature, and directly wanted.

Research question, split into two claims:
  C1 (validation): Content-based sparse attention preserves associative recall
      at matched capacity (vs. the fixed-state alternatives, which fail).
  C2 (novel contribution): A HIERARCHICAL, sub-quadratic selector preserves
      that recall while cutting selection cost — demonstrated on a
      recall-vs-length AND cost-vs-length frontier. C2 is the paper.
      (seq-128 results alone prove nothing about cost scaling — the length
      axis is essential.)


3. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Benchmark: MQAR — the model sees key->value pairs, then queries; exact-match
  recall accuracy. Canonical config so far: input_seq_len=128, num_kv_pairs=8,
  vocab_size=8192.
- Harness: HazyResearch `zoology` (validated public harness; implements the
  baselines and DeepSeek NSA as ready-made mixers). Custom mixers are written
  to be zoology-compatible drop-ins.
- Protocol: matched-capacity frontier — sweep d_model in {64, 128, 256} x
  architecture x 3 learning rates, report best-over-LR recall per point. This
  controls capacity so results reflect ARCHITECTURE, not tuning effort.
- Compute: free Colab / Kaggle T4s (single GPU; sweeps shardable across
  multiple Kaggle sessions). Total project compute budget ~$50-300.
- Cost metric for C2: scored key-value pairs per query (a hardware-independent
  operation count), not formula FLOPs; the prototype uses gather+mask so
  wall-clock is indicative only.


4. RESULTS SO FAR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
M1 — REPRODUCTION OF THE RECALL GAP (done 2026-06-28, committed)
  Attention vs. base_conv (gated convolution) on MQAR, best-over-LR recall:

     d_model | attention | base_conv
     --------+-----------+----------
        64   |   0.998   |   0.097
       128   |   0.996   |   0.127
       256   |   0.996   |   0.131

  - Clean ~0.87 absolute separation, stable across three model sizes.
  - Strength of the result: on base_conv, train loss -> ~0 while val recall
    stays ~0.10 — the gap is architectural, not undertraining.
  - Caveats: attention d512 did not converge within the 48-epoch cap
    (optimization artifact, excluded from headline); base_conv d512 partial
    (runtime died at epoch 15, plateaued ~0.13).
  - Artifacts: experiments/m1_results/ (RESULTS.md, figure, parser script).

M2a — NSA ON THE SAME FRONTIER (done 2026-06-29, committed, commit 9a855bc)
  Added zoology.mixers.deepseek_nsa.SparseAttention as a third curve,
  same protocol, run on Kaggle T4:

     d_model | attention | base_conv |   NSA
     --------+-----------+-----------+-------
        64   |   0.997   |   0.096   | 0.991
       128   |   0.998   |   0.129   | 0.990
       256   |   0.994   |   0.130   | 0.993

  - NSA rides the attention ceiling at all three sizes; base_conv stays at
    the floor. This confirms HALF of the SubQ thesis (C1): content-based
    sparse attention preserves recall at matched capacity.
  - It does NOT confirm the other half: NSA's selector is quadratic (every
    query scores all compressed blocks densely).
  - Observations: NSA converges slower (~45-50 epochs vs. ~10-15 for
    attention) and is more LR-sensitive.
  - Artifacts: experiments/m2_results/ (sweep.log, RESULTS.md, frontier plot).

M2b — HIERARCHICAL SUB-QUADRATIC SELECTOR (designed + coded, NOT YET RUN)
  The novel contribution. Implementation: src/hier_nsa.py
  (HierSparseAttention, a zoology-compatible mixer) and
  experiments/m2b_hier_frontier.py (the length-sweep harness).

  Design (informed by reading lucidrains/native-sparse-attention-pytorch,
  which zoology's NSA mixer wraps):
  - Where NSA's quadratic cost actually lives: the COARSE branch — every
    query attends over all n/cb compressed blocks (O(n^2/cb)); that dense
    score feeds top-k. The top-k itself is not the problem.
  - The fix: replace the dense coarse stage with a TWO-LEVEL TREE.
    Query scores `nsup` super-block summaries (dense but small) -> select
    top-s1 super-blocks -> gather their g member blocks -> top-k fine blocks
    -> exact attention over those. Per-query coarse cost = nsup + s1*g,
    vs. NSA's nblk.
  - Scaling rule: g (super-block group size) must scale as round(sqrt(nblk))
    per sequence length — a fixed g stays O(n^2); with the sqrt rule the
    selector is ~O(n^1.5). The harness sets g per length.
  - Gradient path: hard top-k descent is non-differentiable; gradients reach
    the scoring projections via softmax over the VISITED candidate-block
    scores plus a sigmoid importance gate on the selected blocks.
  - Causality: the coarse stage uses only fully-past blocks (block_end <= t)
    so block-mean summaries never include future tokens; a sliding window
    covers the recent region; branches are zeroed when no past block exists
    (an all-masked softmax otherwise leaks future information — this bug was
    caught and fixed during implementation).

  Experiment plan: recall AND cost vs. sequence length, seq in {128, 512,
  2048} (8192 deferred to a separate experiment), hier vs. NSA vs. attention.
  Harness hardening already committed (2026-06-30): 0.98 early-stop, 40-epoch
  cap, SHARD/NSHARDS env vars for parallel multi-GPU sweeps, per-run OOM
  recovery, L512 batch-size fix.

6. ANTICIPATED OUTCOMES AND HONEST FRAMING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- If hier matches NSA recall at all lengths at ~O(n^1.5) selector cost:
  the paper's claim is "recall-preserving sparse attention does not require a
  quadratic selector" — a concrete, independent, reproducible data point on
  the open SSA/SubQ question.
- If hier loses recall at longer lengths: that is ALSO a publishable negative
  result — "the dense coarse pass is what buys recall; cheap selection
  reintroduces the recall wall" — and directly informs the field's sharpest
  open question (sparse selection vs. selective exact memory).
- Known limitations to state up front: synthetic task only (MQAR; RULER/NIAH
  is a stretch goal), tiny models, single-run-per-cell budget (best-over-LR,
  not error bars over seeds), cost measured in scored-pairs/query rather than
  optimized-kernel wall-clock.
